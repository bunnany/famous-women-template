Dame Whina Cooper

Dame Whina Cooper (1895–1994) was a revered Māori leader and activist whose impact on New Zealand's social and political landscape remains significant. Born in the Hokianga region, she was deeply rooted in her Māori heritage and worked tirelessly to protect and promote Māori culture, language, and land rights. Her leadership was particularly notable in the 1975 Māori Land March, where she, at the age of 79, led thousands of people on a 1,000-kilometer journey from Northland to Wellington. This march drew national attention to the plight of Māori land and became a defining moment in New Zealand’s history, symbolizing the broader struggle for indigenous rights.

Throughout her life, Dame Whina was a staunch advocate for justice, equality, and the empowerment of her people. Her legacy is marked by her unwavering commitment to these causes, which earned her widespread respect and admiration. Known as "Te Whaea o te Motu" (Mother of the Nation), Dame Whina Cooper inspired future generations of Māori and non-Māori alike to continue the fight for social justice and cultural preservation. Her contributions to New Zealand society were recognized with numerous honors, cementing her place as one of the country's most influential figures.
